AI, work and health

Artificial intelligence should be analysed as a transformation of work

From an occupational-health perspective, the central question is not whether a system is labelled “AI”, but how it changes tasks, exposure, discretion, pace, collective regulation and the meaning attached to professional work.

Why a psychosocial-risk framework is useful

The six dimensions identified in the French Gollac framework provide a structured way to examine how AI changes work intensity and time, emotional demands, autonomy, social relations, conflicts of values and job or organisational insecurity.

These dimensions are particularly useful because a single workplace AI system may act on several pathways simultaneously. A tool may remove repetitive tasks while accelerating output expectations, reducing informal cooperation and creating new checking responsibilities.

The framework does not assume that harm is inevitable. Outcomes depend on the use case, degree of system authority, staffing, objectives, training, participation and the resources available for human oversight.

Published reference

Large language models and new psychosocial challenges at work

The article applies the Gollac dimensions to LLM deployment, including intensification, cognitive burden, skill loss, isolation and insecurity.

Archives des Maladies Professionnelles et de l’Environnement, 86, 102877.

Six pathways to examine

How workplace AI can affect psychosocial risks

The mechanisms are not automatic. They depend on system design, organisational choices and the extent to which workers retain meaningful control over their activity.

1 · Work intensity

Acceleration and invisible rework

Expected productivity may increase while the time needed to verify, correct and document AI output remains hidden.

Ask: are theoretical time savings being converted into higher targets?
2 · Emotional demands

Explaining decisions workers do not control

Workers may have to justify automated decisions to clients, patients or colleagues while remaining responsible for difficult consequences.

Ask: who manages complaints, distress and repair when the system is wrong?
3 · Autonomy

Enabling tool or prescriptive system

AI may extend capability when it leaves room for judgement, or reduce discretion when it imposes priorities, methods or pace.

Ask: can workers override the system without penalty?
4 · Social relations

Weakened cooperation and support

AI assistants may replace informal learning, peer discussion or managerial support and create conflict over responsibility for errors.

Ask: what collective regulation or exchange is being removed?
5 · Conflicts of values

Being unable to recognise quality work

Workers may be required to endorse output they consider unsafe, unfair or inconsistent with professional standards.

Ask: which quality criteria cannot be sacrificed to speed?
6 · Insecurity

Jobs, skills and professional identity

Uncertainty may concern employment, changing roles, devaluation of expertise, imposed mobility or dependency on poorly understood systems.

Ask: are future roles and evaluation criteria explained credibly?
Primary prevention

Why involve occupational health before deployment

The purpose is not to certify the technology. It is to identify foreseeable effects on activity and health, then help integrate those effects into collective prevention.

Before solution selection

Define the actual use

Describe the tasks being changed, affected populations, whether use is optional or imposed, the data involved, decisions influenced and situations in which workers may depart from the system’s recommendation.

Work analysis

Characterise exposure

Assess potential intensification, checking workload, changes in autonomy, decision transparency, effects on collective work, surveillance and situations of unclear responsibility.

Pilot and deployment

Monitor early warning signs

Observe errors, team tensions, cognitive overload, loss of meaning, perceived control, workarounds, unequal effects and potential exclusion from work.

Governance

Escalate and adjust

Integrate relevant risks into occupational risk assessment, support worker participation, recognise verification time, document system limits and preserve collective discussion about work quality.

Three risk families

One system can create several forms of exposure

Risks arise from the task, data, level of human oversight, decision authority and organisational use of expected efficiency gains—not from the model alone.

Technical risk

Hallucinations and confident errors

A language model may produce a false answer in polished and authoritative language, including invented sources, incorrect thresholds or an unsuitable clinical or legal recommendation.

A well-written answer is not evidence of reliability.
Organisational risk

Psychosocial and work-design effects

AI can alter pace, discretion, cooperation, values, skill development and insecurity. These effects may be more important than isolated technical performance.

The relevant unit of analysis is the work system.
Governance risk

Surveillance and sensitive data

When systems allocate tasks, score performance, monitor behaviour or infer emotional states, they change workplace power relations and require legal, ethical and medical scrutiny.

Monitoring capability should not become monitoring by default.
Plausible errors

The central LLM hazard: a fluent error that is difficult to detect

A hallucination is not merely poor wording. It is a false claim that may be expressed in correct language, with a coherent structure and an authoritative tone.

What produces the problem

A language model predicts a plausible continuation from training patterns, the prompt and available context. It does not spontaneously verify reality, current law or the facts of a specific workplace situation.

Why the problem is dangerous

Fluent style lowers vigilance. The risk is greatest when AI is used to save time on tasks for which independent verification is slow, specialised or difficult.

Error type

Invented sources

The model may cite a study, guideline, legal provision or standard that does not exist, or combine several real references incorrectly.

Error type

Wrong legal or national context

A valid rule may be applied to the wrong jurisdiction, profession, date or institutional role.

Error type

Clinical overconfidence

The output may be more specific than the available information allows because the real job, exposure, history or record is missing.

Risk factor

Rare and specialised questions

Errors may increase when the question concerns niche regulations, uncommon exposures or unusual medico-legal situations.

Risk factor

Long or ambiguous prompts

Conflicting or poorly prioritised instructions may cause the model to overlook a constraint or answer only part of the request.

Prevention

Organised verification

Use reliable source material, require traceable references, retain version information and prohibit autonomous use for sensitive decisions.

Practical safeguards

Four principles for safer professional use

The level of control should reflect the task. Reformulating a public document does not require the same safeguards as a medical, employment, legal or safety decision.

1

Define the scope

State whether the system supports search, drafting, second reading, classification or a decision.

2

Provide trusted sources

Use official texts, validated guidance and approved internal documents where appropriate.

3

Require verifiability

Request references, uncertainty, limitations and a clear distinction between evidence and inference.

4

Keep competent human control

A qualified professional should validate sensitive output before it affects a person or organisation.

AI-specific mechanisms

Risks that require additional attention

Opacity, supervision burden and skill erosion

Opacity: workers may use or be affected by a system whose criteria and limitations they cannot understand.

Supervision burden: probabilistic output may require continuous checking, doubt and correction.

Trust calibration: users need enough trust to work efficiently while retaining enough distance to detect errors.

Skill erosion: long-term delegation can reduce opportunities to learn, practise and transmit professional judgement.

When AI becomes a management system

Stronger safeguards are needed when AI allocates tasks, sets schedules, measures performance, ranks priorities or monitors activity.

Workers may feel evaluated by criteria they cannot see or challenge. This can reduce discretion, increase self-monitoring and weaken the legitimacy of managerial decisions.

Worker participation, transparent criteria and an effective human appeal route are therefore central prevention measures.
