Risks & prevention · From signal to action

Prevent AI risks where they transform work.

Identify what changes in the activity, make exposure signals visible, and organise preventive measures before effects become established.

Thomas Cole, Destruction, 1836

In 30 seconds

Risk does not come from the model alone.

  • It emerges in a specific situation: a task, people, an organisation, and a degree of authority given to the system.
  • Early effects often involve workload, autonomy, responsibilities, skills, and the way collective work functions.
  • Prevention means acting on use and organisation, then comparing expected effects with those actually observed.
FrameworkSix dimensions of psychosocial risk — Gollac report
AudiencePrevention and occupational health professionals, worker representatives, HR, employers, and project teams
PrincipleAvoid · Reduce · Organise · Monitor

From system to health

Understand how risk takes shape.

A continuous reading avoids two errors: attributing every effect to the technology or, conversely, overlooking the role of organisational choices.

Real-work lens

Organisational and psychosocial framework

AI never creates risk on its own.

Risk takes shape when the system’s capabilities meet a task, objectives, constraints, data, and room for manoeuvre. This encounter is what must be analysed.

Risk pathway

01Work situationTask, people, constraints, and expected outcome
02Authority given to the systemAdvise, rank, impose, monitor, or decide
03Changed organisationWorkload, autonomy, cooperation, and responsibilities
04Observable effectsQuality, safety, health, and continued employment
AvoidReject uses that lack a defensible occupational need.
ReduceLimit authority, data, and exposure situations.
OrganiseProvide time, skills, and meaningful access to human review.
MonitorObserve, correct, suspend, or withdraw when necessary.

Key point: the right unit of analysis is the complete work system — not the tool in isolation.

Six dimensions · A situated reading

See what changes before measuring what goes wrong.

The six dimensions of psychosocial risk remain present, but are grouped into two complementary readings to avoid a catalogue effect.

Organisation of activity01

Workload, autonomy, and collective work.

Observe how the tool redistributes time, room for manoeuvre, and opportunities for cooperation.

Intensity & timeI

Does the expected gain become higher output while verification work remains invisible?

Prevent: measure actual time and adjust objectives.
AutonomyII

Can the professional depart from the recommendation without being penalised or treated with suspicion?

Prevent: guarantee a right to depart and seek review.
Work relationshipsIII

Which exchanges, sources of support, or collective learning disappear when the assistant comes between people?

Prevent: maintain time for collective work regulation.
Experience of work02

Emotion, values, and occupational pathways.

Identify what the tool changes in relationships with others, standards of good work, and occupational futures.

Emotional demandsIV

Who must explain, repair, or assume responsibility for a decision they do not fully control?

Prevent: plan support, review, and remediation.
Value conflictsV

Does the professional still recognise the quality of what they produce, validate, or sign?

Prevent: set non-negotiable quality criteria.
InsecurityVI

Are future roles, expected skills, and assessment criteria explicit?

Prevent: clarify pathways and organise training.

Cross-cutting case · Language models

When a plausible error enters a decision.

A hallucination is not an occupational risk in itself. It becomes one when a false but convincing output is used without the resources required to verify it.

Two moments · Two decisions

Act before. Verify after.

The employer organises prevention with workers and their representatives. The occupational health service clarifies effects on work and health; legal, data, and security expertise complete the analysis.

Before deployment01

Describe, test, and make reversible.

Start with an occupational problem and a documented baseline, not with a technological promise.

DescribeTask, people, data, decisions, and those who may be affected.
TestLimited pilot, explicit criteria, and representative difficult situations.
PlanVerification time, fallback solution, stop thresholds, and responsibilities.
