Understand · Essential concepts

Understand artificial intelligence at work.

Distinguish between AI systems, follow how they work, and connect each concept to the changes it brings to tasks, decisions and responsibilities.

Start reading ↓

In 30 seconds

The essentials for understanding AI at work.

  • “AI” covers several types of system that produce neither the same outputs nor the same risks.
  • A language model generates a plausible sequence; it does not independently validate truth or compliance.
  • The relevant unit of analysis remains the work system: tool, task, data, people and organisation.

What this changes at work

Each category changes a different part of the work system.

Organisational perspective
Tasks

Generation adds review; prediction adds interpretation; algorithmic management directly changes how work is carried out.

Organisation

Validation pathways, alert thresholds and ways to challenge outcomes must be designed differently.

Health and skills

Review workload, loss of autonomy and potential deskilling must be linked to the type of system.

02Follow the flow

How a language model produces an answer.

It does not independently consult a database of truths. It progressively generates a plausible sequence from its training, the prompt and the available context.

Process diagram · simplified view4 steps · 1 output to verify
01

Prompt

The request, context and supplied documents shape the answer.

02

Patterns

The model draws on statistical relationships learned during training.

03

Prediction

It progressively selects the most plausible continuation in that context.

04

Output

The answer may sound convincing while remaining inaccurate or unsuitable.

Key point: this sequence is probabilistic. It is neither human reasoning nor a verification of truth.

Real-work perspective

A model’s output is never the whole of the work.

In care as in prevention, work involves a situation, relationships, trade-offs and responsibility. Technology enters this fabric; it does not sum it up.

What this changes at work

Verification becomes a task in its own right.

Work-activity perspective
Tasks

Time saved on production may be transferred to prompting, review, source checking and correction.

Organisation

It must be clear who verifies, using what information, within what timeframe and with what authority to reject the output.

Health and skills

Supervision without time or expertise creates hidden workload and an unsustainable burden of responsibility.

03Compare

What a tool can do — and what it does not guarantee.

A system can be useful without being reliable enough to decide. Assessment must remain tied to a task, a level of risk and a specific context.

Key point: linguistic plausibility is not evidence, a compliance guarantee, or clinical or legal validation.

What this changes at work

Tool performance does not predict the quality of work.

Critical perspective
Tasks

A faster task may create new checks, corrections and manual rework when errors occur.

Organisation

Claimed gains must be compared with review time, the cost of errors and situations the tool does not cover.

Health and skills

Use may support work or weaken the profession depending on the place given to expertise and room for manoeuvre.

04Decide

Five questions before professional use.

The relevant unit of analysis is not the model alone, but the whole system: tool, data, users, organisation, responsibilities and effects on real work.

InputReal task
MaterialData
ProcessingAI system
ActionHuman decision
EffectsWork & health
01Which task is actually being transformed?+
Describe work before and after the tool, including added checks, edge cases and manual rework.
02What data is being used?+
Identify personal, medical and HR data or activity records, along with their quality and retention period.
03Who can review the output?+
Ensure reviewers genuinely have the necessary time, skills, information and authority.
04Which responsibilities are shifting?+
Identify who decides, who validates, who can challenge and who bears the consequences of an error.
05Which effects will be monitored?+
Define indicators covering quality, workload, autonomy, errors, skills and working collectives.

What this changes at work

Prevention begins before the tool is selected.

Design perspective
Tasks

Describing real activity helps prevent a tool optimised for an abstract scenario from disrupting actual work.

Organisation

The pilot must make responsibilities, stop criteria, appeal routes and social dialogue arrangements visible.

Health and skills

Involving the occupational health and prevention service upstream helps anticipate effects before they become individualised.
