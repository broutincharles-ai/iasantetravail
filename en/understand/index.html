Understanding artificial intelligence at work

Distinguish the main types of AI systems, understand how a large language model produces an answer, and identify what no system can guarantee before it is used in a professional setting.

Explore the fundamentals
In 30 seconds

Essential points

  • “AI” covers different systems: generative AI, predictive or decision-support systems, and algorithmic management.
  • A large language model generates a plausible sequence of tokens; it does not inherently verify truth, legality or clinical relevance.
  • The useful question is not only what the tool can produce, but how it changes tasks, decisions, workload and responsibility.

Start with the right category

Three forms of AI that can transform work

Before assessing a system, identify what kind of system it is. These categories have different purposes, data flows, failure modes and consequences for workers.

1

Generative AI and large language models

These systems produce or transform text, images, code and summaries. Fluency can create an impression of understanding, but a coherent answer may still be factually or contextually wrong.

Examples: drafting, summarising, translating, extracting and brainstorming.
Learn more

How generative AI works

A generative model learns statistical regularities from large datasets, then produces new content in response to an instruction and the context supplied at use time.

For a large language model

  • Text is split into units called tokens.
  • The model estimates which tokens are most likely to follow the current context.
  • The answer is generated progressively rather than retrieved from a verified catalogue of facts.
  • Output quality depends on the prompt, available context, training, model configuration and connected tools.

What to monitor at work

  • Fabricated facts, false references and unjustified confidence.
  • Disclosure of confidential, personal or health data.
  • Standardisation of professional writing and erosion of skills.
  • The actual time required to verify outputs.

Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Mistral or an internal assistant connected to organisational documents.

2

Predictive and decision-support systems

These systems score, classify, detect, rank or recommend. Their reliability depends on the target being predicted, the training data, the selected threshold and the consequences of error.

Examples: recruitment, scheduling, risk detection and workload forecasting.
Learn more

How predictive systems work

The system learns relationships between input data and an expected output such as a probability, category, ranking or recommendation.

Typical steps

  • Define the event, score or decision the system is intended to support.
  • Train or configure the system using historical or labelled data.
  • Select an alert or decision threshold.
  • Measure errors and monitor performance after deployment.

What to monitor at work

  • Bias embedded in historical data or proxy variables.
  • False positives and false negatives with unequal consequences.
  • Scores that affected workers or professionals cannot understand or challenge.
  • Gradual automation of a decision that was supposed to remain human.

Examples: applicant scoring, absence prediction, safety alerts, workload forecasting or allocation recommendations.

3

Algorithmic management

Algorithmic management uses data and automated rules to allocate, monitor or evaluate work. The central issue is not only technical accuracy, but its effects on autonomy, pace, cooperation and accountability.

Examples: task allocation, performance monitoring, automated scheduling and worker scoring.
Learn more

What algorithmic management does

It uses data, rules and sometimes machine-learning models to organise, measure or influence workers and their activity.

Common functions

  • Automatically allocate tasks, shifts, routes or priorities.
  • Track time, movement, objectives or performance indicators.
  • Rank workers or trigger managerial alerts.
  • Recommend or impose pace, sequencing or a course of action.

What to monitor at work

  • Work intensification and reduced discretion.
  • Continuous monitoring or a persistent sense of surveillance.
  • Targets derived from incomplete indicators.
  • Weakened collective work and unclear responsibility when the system is wrong.

Examples: route optimisation, automated scheduling, productivity tracking, dynamic task allocation or performance ranking.

A useful mental model

How a large language model produces an answer

An LLM does not spontaneously consult a verified database of truths. It generates a response from learned statistical patterns, the instruction, the available context and any tools connected to it.

1

An instruction

The prompt, context and documents supplied by the user shape the task.

2

Learned patterns

The model uses statistical relationships acquired during training and adaptation.

3

Progressive prediction

It generates the most plausible continuation token by token within the current context.

4

An output to verify

The answer can be useful and convincing while remaining incomplete, outdated or incorrect.

Key point: linguistic plausibility is not evidence, regulatory compliance, clinical validation or professional judgement.

Critical reading

What AI can support — and what it cannot guarantee

A system may be useful without being sufficiently reliable to decide. Performance must always be interpreted in relation to a specific task, population, consequence and working environment.

AI may help to…

  • Structure or summarise a large body of documents.
  • Reformulate content for different audiences.
  • Flag inconsistencies or points requiring review.
  • Prepare an evidence scan or an initial analytical framework.
  • Accelerate bounded tasks under competent human supervision.

It does not guarantee…

  • That an answer is true, complete or up to date.
  • Compliance with data-protection, AI or employment law.
  • The absence of bias in data, thresholds or outputs.
  • The suitability of an individual medical, legal or employment decision.
  • A positive effect on workload, autonomy, skills or collective work.
Occupational health and safety

Five questions before AI is used professionally

The correct unit of analysis is not the model alone. It is the full work system: tool, data, users, organisation, responsibilities and effects on real work.

Question 1

Which task is changing?

Describe work before and after introduction, rather than listing only the system’s features.

Question 2

Which data are involved?

Identify personal, health, employment and behavioural data, including data inferred from activity.

Question 3

Who can verify the output?

Ensure that oversight comes with sufficient time, competence, information and authority.

Question 4

How does responsibility move?

Clarify who recommends, decides, validates, challenges and bears the consequences of error.

Question 5

Which effects will be monitored?

Define indicators for quality, error, workload, autonomy, skills, cooperation and worker experience.

+

Involve occupational health and safety expertise before deployment. Early involvement helps identify how the system may transform work before difficulties become individual complaints, organisational conflict or health effects.

Useful concepts without overloading the main pathway

RAG and agents: two developments to understand

Retrieval-augmented generation

RAG allows a model to search a document collection before answering. It may reduce some fabrications, but it does not remove the need to verify the documents, their date, scope and interpretation.

AI agents

An agent combines a model with tools, memory and sometimes the ability to act in software. The more independently it acts, the more important logging, permissions, human validation and stop conditions become.

How to formulate a professional request

A better prompt can improve the request and the output format. It cannot make an unsafe use safe by itself.

Define the task

State the objective, intended audience and expected format.

Provide useful context

Specify the country, professional setting, time frame and required level of detail.

Require sources

Distinguish supplied documents, general knowledge and assumptions.

Make uncertainty visible

Ask the model to identify missing, doubtful or unverifiable points.

Protect data

Do not enter identifiable or sensitive data into an unapproved system.

Test several cases

Look for recurring errors and document the model, configuration and workflow tested.
