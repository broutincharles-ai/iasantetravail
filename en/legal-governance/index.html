Govern workplace AI before deployment

EU AI Act, GDPR, worker consultation and occupational-risk prevention must be connected to the system’s actual use, the data processed and its effects on work.

Understand the applicable framework
In 30 seconds

Essential points

  • The legal and governance framework depends on the actual workplace use: documentation, recruitment, task allocation, monitoring, health or a decision affecting a person.
  • The EU AI Act, GDPR, labour law, worker consultation and occupational-risk prevention are cumulative; compliance with one does not replace the others.
  • Governance should define purpose, authority, data, human oversight, evidence, worker involvement, incident handling and conditions for stopping the system.

Start from the real use

Use a decision architecture, not a fixed compliance table

The same model may be low-impact when reformulating public text, sensitive when processing employee data, high-risk when used in recruitment or worker management, and unacceptable when used for prohibited workplace emotion recognition.

The legal reflex

Do not begin with the brand name or technical category alone. Describe the purpose, people affected, data, output, authority given to the system and consequences of an error.

The applicable rules then emerge from concrete questions: does the system process personal or health data, influence employment decisions, allocate work, monitor activity or create a significant automated decision?

The prevention reflex

Legal permission is not the end of the analysis

A system may remain problematic because of workload, autonomy, skills, surveillance, unequal effects or unclear responsibility.

What does the AI system actually do in real work?

Four cumulative layers

EU AI Act, GDPR, labour law and occupational-risk prevention

These frameworks answer different questions and may all apply to the same project.

European Union

EU AI Act: obligations depend on the use and risk level

Workplace-relevant provisions include prohibited practices, transparency duties, rules for general-purpose AI and high-risk requirements for certain employment and worker-management systems.

High-risk safeguards include risk management, data governance, logging, documentation, information for deployers, human oversight, accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity.

Employment, recruitment and worker-management systems require specific classification rather than generic approval of the model.
Personal data

GDPR: purpose, minimisation, transparency and rights

When AI processes data about workers, candidates, patients or users, organisations must identify a lawful basis, limit the purpose and data, define retention, ensure security and enable the exercise of rights.

Health, biometric and inferred psychological data, large-scale monitoring and automated decisions require particular caution. A data-protection impact assessment may be necessary.

Provider documentation does not replace the deployer’s own GDPR analysis.
Worker participation

Labour law and consultation: involve workers early enough to matter

Applicable national rules vary. A sound governance process gives workers and their representatives information on purpose, tasks, data, criteria, risks, safeguards and alternatives before the project is effectively deployed.

In France, specific CSE information and consultation duties apply to new technologies, automated personnel management, recruitment methods and employee-monitoring techniques.

A consultation held after general rollout cannot influence the design.
Occupational health and safety

Prevention duties: treat AI as a transformation of work

Employers must protect physical and mental health and assess occupational risks. AI projects may require preventive action when they change pace, autonomy, surveillance, skills, emotional demands or work organisation.

In France, relevant risks should be reflected in the occupational risk assessment document and associated prevention programme where applicable.

Training alone is not sufficient when the system or workflow itself creates the risk.
Practical use matrix

Start from what the AI system does at work

The classification below is indicative. The exact obligations depend on the data, affected people, decision authority and national context.

Document assistance

Search, summarisation and reformulation

Usually lower impact when no personal, confidential or protected information is shared. Main safeguards include confidentiality, source verification, traceability and appropriate disclosure of AI-assisted content.

Human resources

Recruitment, scoring, promotion and training

Potentially high-risk under the EU AI Act. GDPR, bias testing, meaningful human oversight, candidate or employee information and worker consultation may also apply.

Work organisation

Scheduling, task allocation and prioritisation

A governance turning point. The system no longer merely assists an individual: it may prescribe or organise activity. Criteria, appeal routes and effects on workload, autonomy, deadlines and cooperation should be documented.

Monitoring and control

Performance measurement and activity analysis

Highly sensitive. Monitoring should be necessary, proportionate, transparent and subject to applicable labour and data-protection rules. Workplace emotion recognition is prohibited under the EU AI Act, subject to narrow exceptions defined by the regulation.

Occupational health

Health data, follow-up and decision support

Maximum caution. Medical confidentiality, health data, security, hosting, separation from employer access and competent medical supervision are central. Decision support should not become an autonomous decision on health or fitness for work.

Collective prevention

Risk assessment and weak-signal analysis

Promising but context-dependent. AI may help structure information or identify patterns, but risk qualification should remain multidisciplinary, contextual and discussed with people who understand the real work.

EU AI Act timeline

A progressive and recently revised timetable

The 2026 simplification regulation delayed the application of high-risk requirements while leaving earlier prohibitions, AI-literacy duties and general-purpose AI rules in place.

02/2025

Prohibited practices and AI-literacy obligations began to apply.

08/2025

Governance provisions and obligations for general-purpose AI models became applicable.

08/2026

General application of the regulation and core transparency duties, depending on the provision concerned.

12/2027

High-risk rules for stand-alone systems, including certain employment and worker-management uses.

08/2028

High-risk rules for systems embedded in regulated products.

Worker participation

Consultation should enable workers to influence the project

A useful consultation addresses the actual effects of the system, not only a technical or marketing presentation.

Timing

Consult before implementation is effectively determined

A pilot may already amount to implementation when employees use the tool in organised conditions, training is scheduled or wider rollout has been decided.

Information

Provide enough evidence to understand the change

Documentation should cover purpose, affected groups, transformed tasks, data, criteria, human oversight, expected employment and workload effects, alternatives and pilot results.

Expert support

Allow independent analysis where the project is significant

National law may allow worker representatives to obtain expert support. In France, the CSE may use an authorised expert in defined cases involving new technology or a major change to health, safety or working conditions.

Content of the opinion

Discuss safeguards and follow-up, not only the purchase decision

Worker representatives may examine transparency, skills, checking time, actual workload, surveillance, training, escalation routes and the conditions for reassessing or stopping the system.

French-specific note

For companies within the relevant statutory scope, Articles L.2312-8 and L.2312-38 of the French Labour Code address new technologies, automated personnel management, recruitment methods and employee-monitoring techniques. Other countries use different consultation structures.

Governance lifecycle

Six decisions to document from idea to follow-up

Governance is a continuing process rather than a one-time compliance review.

1

Define the purpose

State the workplace problem, intended benefit, affected people and prohibited uses.

2

Classify the system

Identify AI Act status, data categories, decision effects and national labour-law requirements.

3

Assess the work impact

Examine tasks, workload, autonomy, skills, cooperation, surveillance and psychosocial risks.

4

Consult and test

Involve affected workers, representatives, DPO, occupational health, legal and security expertise.

5

Authorise with conditions

Define oversight, data access, logging, incident handling, appeal, fallback and stop criteria.

6

Monitor and reassess

Review errors, overrides, unequal effects, workload, system changes and reasons to redesign or stop.

French case law

An emerging line of decisions remains highly fact-dependent

Recent French disputes increasingly examine whether AI pilots and tools should have been subject to prior CSE consultation. The outcome depends on the actual use, maturity of deployment and demonstrated effects on working conditions.

This section is informational and France-specific. Interim orders and first-instance judgments may be appealed and should not be treated as universal rules.
French Supreme Court · 2018

AI is not automatically a major project

In a dispute concerning an IBM Watson tool supporting email processing, the court accepted a concrete analysis of the consequences. Where direct effects on working conditions were considered limited, expert assistance could be refused.

Cass. soc., 12 April 2018, no. 16-27.866.
Nanterre · interim proceedings 2025

A pilot may already be an implementation

A project described as experimental was treated as already implemented where workers were using the tools and deployment had been organised. This does not make every test automatically consultable; maturity and effects remain relevant.

TJ Nanterre, 14 February 2025, no. 24/01457.
Paris Court of Appeal · 2026

Suspension confirmed pending consultation

The court confirmed that introducing generative-AI tools into editorial work could constitute a new technology affecting working conditions and justify prior CSE consultation.

TJ Créteil, 15 July 2025, no. 25/00851; CA Paris, 21 May 2026, no. 25/13232.
Paris · interim proceedings 2025

A new AI platform may differ from a routine update

The decision distinguished access to a new platform offering generative-AI tools from a modification to an existing HR chatbot where no new employee effect was demonstrated.

TJ Paris, 2 September 2025, no. 25/53278.
Occupational-health contribution

What occupational health adds to legal governance

Occupational-health teams do not replace the employer’s legal analysis, the DPO or worker representatives. Their contribution is to make effects on real work visible.

Before deployment

Identify the workers concerned, transformed tasks, whether use is optional or imposed, expected checking time, error handling, skill preservation and whether the tool produces or influences a decision affecting a person.

After deployment

Monitor intensification, loss of autonomy, workarounds, hypervigilance, conflicts of values, isolation, disengagement, team tension, errors, perceived surveillance and employment insecurity.

Minimum governance record

What should be documented before authorisation

The precise file will depend on the use and jurisdiction, but these items form a practical baseline.

Purpose, owner and affected populations
Tasks, decisions and prohibited uses
AI Act classification and applicable dates
Personal-data roles and lawful basis
DPIA or other impact assessments where required
Worker information and consultation
Human oversight and appeal route
Testing, error and bias evidence
Occupational-health and safety assessment
Logging, security and access control
Incident, fallback and stop procedures
Review date and change-management trigger
