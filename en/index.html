A work-centred perspective

Why occupational health must be involved in workplace AI

Artificial intelligence is not only a technical tool. Once introduced into an organisation, it may change how work is allocated, performed, monitored, verified and valued. Occupational health brings the analysis back to real tasks, exposure, professional discretion and the conditions required to preserve health.

1

Work design

Identify which tasks disappear, which tasks become more complex and where the burden of checking AI output is placed.

2

Psychosocial risks

Assess intensification, reduced autonomy, emotional demands, conflicts of values, weakened cooperation and insecurity.

3

Human oversight

Determine whether workers have the time, competence, information and authority to challenge or reject an automated recommendation.

4

Long-term monitoring

Measure errors, rework, workload, skill changes and unintended organisational effects after deployment.
